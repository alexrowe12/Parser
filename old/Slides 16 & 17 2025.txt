THE THREE LAWS OF ROBOTICS

“AS A MACHINE, A ROBOT WILL SURELY BE DESIGNED FOR
SAFETY, AS FAR AS POSSIBLE.” (8)

¢° 1/ A ROBOT MAY NOT INJURE A HUMAN BEING OR, THROUGH
INACTION, ALLOW A HUMAN BEING TO COME TO HARM.

¢ 2/ A ROBOT MUST OBEY THE ORDERS GIVEN TO IT BY HUMAN
BEINGS, EXCEPT WHERE SUCH ORDERS WOULD CONFLICT WITH THE
FIRST LAW.

¢ 3/ A ROBOT MUST PROTECT ITS OWN EXISTENCE AS LONG AS SUCH
PROTECTION DOES NOT CONFLICT WITH THE FIRST OR SECOND LAW.

These three laws are Asimov's antidotes
against the Frankenstein complex.

Conclusion
AS LONG AS REAL CONSCIOUS ROBOTS DO

NOT EXIST, SCI-FI ROBOT CHARACTERS WILL
RISK REMAINING THE MERE AND POOR
MIRRORS IN WHICH HUMANS CAN CONSIDER
WHO THEY WOULD LIKE TO BE OR WHO THEY
WOULD DISLIKE BECOMING. INDEED,
ASIMOV'S STORIES ARE NOT REALLY ABLE TO
DESCRIBE THE SINGULARITY, EVEN FICTITIOUS,
OF CONSCIOUS ROBOTS: ALL HIS STORIES TRY
TO KNOW WHETHER OR NOT IT’S POSSIBLE TO
CALL A ROBOT A PERSON, THAT IS TO SAY —
FOR ASIMOV — A HUMAN PERSON. ALL HIS
STORIES DEAL WITH ROBOTS THAT ARE ABLE TO
BE MAD, OR RATIONAL, OR FEAR DEATH, LIKE
HUMAN BEINGS DO, LIKE HUMAN PERSONS
DO. ALL THESE ROBOTS ARE HUMAN, VERY
HUMAN. MAYBE, AS NIETZSCHE MIGHT SAY,
ALL TOO HUMAN.
